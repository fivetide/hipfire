A four-stroke internal combustion engine operates through a repeating cycle of four distinct phases: intake, compression, power, and exhaust. During the intake stroke, the piston moves downward from top dead center, creating a partial vacuum that draws a mixture of air and fuel into the cylinder through the open intake valve. The ratio of air to fuel is carefully controlled by the engine management system to ensure efficient combustion.

In the compression stroke, both valves close and the piston moves upward, compressing the air-fuel mixture to approximately one-eighth to one-twelfth of its original volume. This compression raises both the temperature and pressure of the mixture significantly. The compression ratio is a key factor in determining engine efficiency, with higher ratios generally producing more power but requiring higher-octane fuel to prevent premature detonation.

The power stroke begins when the spark plug ignites the compressed mixture near top dead center. The resulting combustion produces hot expanding gases that force the piston downward with considerable force. This is the only stroke that produces positive work output; the other three strokes are powered by the momentum of the crankshaft and flywheel. The combustion process itself takes only a few milliseconds but generates temperatures exceeding 2000 degrees Celsius and pressures of 40 to 60 bar.

Finally, during the exhaust stroke, the exhaust valve opens and the piston moves upward again, pushing the spent combustion gases out of the cylinder and into the exhaust manifold. The cycle then repeats, with the crankshaft converting the linear motion of the pistons into rotational motion that ultimately drives the wheels through the transmission system.

Modern fuel injection systems have largely replaced carburetors in automotive engines. Port fuel injection sprays fuel into the intake port just upstream of the intake valve, where it mixes with incoming air before entering the cylinder. Direct injection systems spray fuel directly into the combustion chamber at high pressure, typically between 50 and 200 bar. This allows more precise control over the amount and timing of fuel delivery, improving both power output and fuel efficiency while reducing harmful emissions.
